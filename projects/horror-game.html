Unreal Engine Horror Game

Unreal Engine 5 · Materials · Shaders · Blueprints · State Trees · PCG · Optimization

Overview

This project is a first-person horror game developed in Unreal Engine 5 with a strong focus on atmosphere, real-time lighting, and performance-conscious systems. My primary contributions centered on technical art workflows, including materials, procedural systems, lighting, optimization, and AI integration.

Gameplay / Visual Demo

Gameplay footage demonstrating lighting, materials, procedural environment systems, and AI-driven character behavior.

Technical Focus Areas

  • Custom material and shader workflows
  • Real-time lighting and atmosphere control
  • Procedural environment generation (PCG)
  • AI state logic and animation integration
  • Performance optimization and debugging

Materials & Shaders

I developed and iterated on custom materials to support the horror atmosphere while remaining performance-conscious. This included controlling roughness, emissive values, and texture usage to ensure consistent lighting behavior across environments. Material instances were used extensively to allow rapid iteration without recompiling base shaders.

Lighting & Atmosphere

Lighting played a critical role in defining the tone of the experience. I worked with dynamic lighting, exposure control, and fog to guide player attention and create tension. Care was taken to balance visual quality with real-time performance constraints.

Procedural Systems & PCG

Procedural Content Generation was used to populate environments efficiently. I tuned density, culling, and transform logic to maintain visual consistency while avoiding unnecessary performance costs. Iteration speed and scalability were key considerations in these systems.

AI, Animation & State Logic

NPC behavior was implemented using Unreal’s State Tree system, integrating movement, animation states, and gameplay triggers. I focused on ensuring clean transitions between states and reliable communication between animation, AI, and gameplay logic.

Optimization & Problem Solving

Throughout development, I identified and addressed performance bottlenecks related to lighting, materials, and procedural systems. Debugging tools and profiling were used to guide decisions such as simplifying materials, adjusting PCG density, and refining update logic.

What I Learned / What I’d Do Next

This project reinforced the importance of technical art as a balance between visual quality and performance. Given more time, I would further expand tooling for faster iteration and explore additional shader-driven effects to enhance atmosphere without significant runtime cost.
